How pure should Christians be?

How to apply "being in the world, but not of it"


This article has been adapted from an essay I wrote for a school assignment.


Origin

During Jesus’ prayer in the garden of Gethsemane, recorded in John 17:1-26, Jesus spoke to the Father, and pleaded with Him for many things. Among His prayer lies the origin of the phrase “in the world but not of it”; Jesus says:

I have given them thy word; and the world hath hated them, because they are not of the world, even as I am not of the world. I pray not that thou shouldest take them out of the world, but that thou shouldest keep them from the evil. They are not of the world, even as I am not of the world. Sanctify them through thy truth: thy word is truth. As thou hast sent me into the world, even so have I also sent them into the world.
— John 17:14-18 KJV

There is so much truth in just these four verses, you may write a whole sermon on them alone – maybe even one for each of them – but what I want to focus on is this repeated phrase that we are not of the world.

Christ is of the Father

Indeed, Christ is not of the world. Christ is of the Father, He is His only begotten Son – "Thou art my Son; this day have I begotten thee" (Psalms 19:2b), "I will be his father, and he shall be my Son" (2 Samuel 7:14a) – likewise, being made anew in Him (2 Corinthians 5:17) and with Him living in us (Galatians 2:20), neither are we of the world, but are made co-heirs with Christ (Romans 8:17, Titus 3:7), and citizens of heaven (Philippians 3:20). By His blood we are reconciled with God (Romans 5:10). Just as the Father sent Christ into the world, "As thou hast sent me into the world", Christ also bought us (1 Corinthians 6:20) and sent us into the world, "even so have I also sent them into the world", to do His bidding and glorify Him. As 1 Corinthians 11:3 states, "the head of the woman is man, and the head of the man is Christ" – Paul lays out this God-glorifying system wherein the woman passes up her glory to the husband, and the husband to Christ, and Christ to the Father.

Why are we here?

That is our purpose here; that is the reason why we are kept in the world, why Jesus prayed that we would not be taken out. We are here for His purpose, for His glory, as we were created by Him and for Him (Colossians 1:16). Of course we cannot do this by ourselves, that is why Jesus promised us and the Father sent forth the great Helper or Comforter in Jesus’ name – the Holy Spirit of God, who strengthens us and gives us power to be witnesses of Christ (Acts 1:8). We are in the world to be the salt and light (Matthew 5:13-16), to be ambassadors of Christ and of heaven (2 Corinthians 5:20, Ephesians 6:20), to show to others the love of the Father by loving Him and each other (1 John 4:17-21), and of course to make disciples of all nations (Matthew 28:18-20). Thus, we are in the world, for a great reason, even while we are not of it. As Jesus said, "If ye were of the world, the world would love his own: but because ye are not of the world, but I have chosen out of the world, therefore the world hateth you" (John 15:19).

How to apply this

This begs the question, "how does this apply to the practical lives of Christians today?" Well, we may look at the rest of the New Testament to see how the Apostles put this into practice. In 1 Corinthians 8, Paul acknowledges that idols are nothing, saying "As concerning therefore the eating of those things that are offered in sacrifice unto idols, we know that an idol is nothing in the world, and that there is none other God but one." (1 Corinthians 8:4). This is the Christian confirmation that there is nothing wrong with the eating of meat given to idols, because idols are nothing. Today, lots of meat you buy in shops or at restaurants is halal, it is offered up to the Muslim god before being slain. We know that there is nothing wrong with eating these meats, as they were offered up to nothing; not to mention we also pray God’s blessing upon the food. However, for a man recently converted from Islam to Christianity, his conscience may be greatly defiled at eating this meat which he for so long ate to please the false god. Thus, the more knowledgeable among us ought not eat that meat and flaunt our liberty to do so, "take heed lest by any means this liberty of yours become a sublingblock to them that are weak" (1 Corinthians 8:9), lest our brother err. Note that Paul’s only contention with the eating of this meat is out of love and concern for the weaker brother, not an objection for God’s sake – "meat commendeth us not to God: for neither, if we eat, are we the better; neither, if we eat not, are we the worse (1 Corinthians 8:8). Thus, as it pertains to meat offered to idols, Christians need not worry about eating it, and may enjoy the food, provided they have prayed God’s blessing upon it, and the eating thereof does not defile another brother’s conscience.

What is not allowed?

That said, there are clearly situations Christians should not participate in. In 1 Peter 4, Peter writes about us, in times past, participating in the ways of the Gentiles, walking in "lasciviousness, lusts, excess of wine, revellings, banquetings, and abominable idolatries" (1 Peter 4:3). The Greek word translated as "banquetings" literally means "drinking parties". These are things which are wrong to all men of God, whether they defile their consciences or not, as they are abominable to the Lord. You may raise the question of living under grace not law, and I would agree that we are dead to sin, but nonetheless we are called to be consecrated peoples, in a passage I will get more into soon. Thus I must conclude that the Bible certainly condemns participating in many of the ways of the culture; today we see a world of rampant sexual immorality, lies, idols, hate, but the Lord commands us to "have no fellowship with the unfruitful works of darkness, but rather reprove them" (Ephesians 5:11).

What are we to do?

The verses I referenced earlier, about being called to be a consecrated people, is Romans 12:1-2: "I beseech you therefore, brethren, by the mercies of God, that ye present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy, acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service. And be not conformed to this world, but be ye transformed by the renewing of your mind, that ye may prove what is that good, and acceptable, and perfect, will of God." "The Christian is like a ship which is made to float in the water", said Pastor Chuck Smith, "As long as it’s floating on the water, it’s alright. The only danger is when you get water in the ship." We are God’s chosen (1 Peter 2:9, John 15:16) and holy people, ordained before time to be here for His purpose (2 Timothy 1:9), to do good works in Christ Jesus (Ephesians 2:10), to love the Lord with all our heart and soul (Mark 12:30), to love one another (Mark 12:31), to be hearers and doers of the Word (James 1:19-27), and to live up to our calling (1 Corinthians 7:20) by serving God in every thing we do (Colossians 3:23). We are to abhor and abstain from evil, and cleave to that which is good (Romans 12:9), for all things which are good are of God (James 1:17, Romans 8:28) but all things which are in the world – the lust of the flesh, the lust of the eyes, the pride of life – are not of the Father (1 John 2:15-17).

Conclusion

We are in the world, on a special commission from the Lord Jesus, for as long as the Holy Spirit shall restrain the evil (2 Thessalonians 2:6-7) and until the Father sends His Son (Matthew 24:26). We have been given plenty to do in the meantime! Do not allow the love of the world to come into you, but follow the Word of God in the Lord Jesus Christ, letting Him reign in you (John 14:20, Colossians 3:15).
